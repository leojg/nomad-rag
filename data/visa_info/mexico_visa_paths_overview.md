## Tourist Stay (FMM / Visitor Permit)

Most remote workers and short-term travelers enter Mexico under the standard visitor permit, historically known as the Forma Migratoria Múltiple (FMM). Since the digitization of border processes, this permit is often issued electronically or stamped directly into your passport at the port of entry.

- Typically grants up to **180 days** in a single stay, though the actual duration is at the discretion of the immigration officer at the border.
- Covers tourism, leisure, and short business visits — not formal local employment.
- No prior application is required for citizens of many countries, including the United States, Canada, the UK, and most EU nations.
- Remote workers earning income from foreign employers or clients generally fall into a gray area under this permit, though it remains the most common entry path used in practice.

It's worth noting that the number of days granted can vary significantly. Travelers have reported receiving anywhere from 30 to 180 days. Arriving with a return ticket, hotel bookings, or proof of funds may help support a longer stamp, though nothing is guaranteed.

## Extending Your Stay

If you entered on a visitor permit and find yourself wanting more time, options for extending within Mexico exist but come with some friction.

- Extensions can be requested through the **Instituto Nacional de Migración (INM)**, Mexico's immigration authority, at local offices across the country.
- The process typically requires completing forms, paying a fee, and providing supporting documents such as your passport, current permit, and proof of financial solvency.
- Extensions are generally granted up to the original 180-day maximum — meaning if you were given 90 days at the border, you may be able to request the remaining 90 days.
- Processing times and requirements can vary by office location and individual officer discretion.

Many long-term visitors choose instead to do a border run — exiting to a neighboring country and re-entering to receive a fresh permit. This approach is common but not officially sanctioned as a long-term strategy, and immigration officers may ask questions if it becomes a repeated pattern.

## Temporary Resident Visa (Residente Temporal)

For those planning to stay longer than 180 days or who want a more stable legal footing, the Temporary Resident Visa is a widely used pathway.

- Valid for **one to four years**, renewable, and issued for purposes including remote work, retirement, family ties, or investment.
- Must typically be applied for at a **Mexican consulate in your home country** before you arrive — you generally cannot convert a tourist entry into temporary residency from inside Mexico without leaving first.
- Common qualifying criteria include demonstrating sufficient financial means, such as a minimum average monthly bank balance or regular income streams. Specific thresholds are set by individual consulates and can change periodically.
- Holders of this visa can open bank accounts, sign leases more easily, and establish a longer-term presence in the country.

- Key documents often requested include:
  - Valid passport with sufficient remaining validity
  - Completed visa application form
  - Proof of economic solvency (bank statements, pay stubs, or pension documentation)
  - Passport-size photographs
  - Consulate fee payment

This visa does **not** automatically grant the right to work for a Mexican employer, but it is generally considered compatible with remote work for foreign companies or clients.

## Permanent Residency

While not the immediate focus for most newcomers, permanent residency is worth knowing about as a longer-term horizon.

- Accessible after holding temporary residency for four consecutive years, or more quickly through family connections to Mexican nationals or through meeting higher financial thresholds.
- Provides an open-ended right to live in Mexico without renewal obligations.

## General Considerations

- Immigration rules in Mexico can shift, and individual consulate interpretations vary — checking directly with the nearest Mexican consulate before travel is always a practical step.
- This overview is intended as a general orientation only and does not constitute legal or immigration advice.
- Community forums, expat groups in cities like Mexico City, Oaxaca, and Playa del Carmen, and licensed immigration attorneys can be valuable resources for up-to-date, situation-specific guidance.
