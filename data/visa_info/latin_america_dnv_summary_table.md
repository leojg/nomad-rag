## Overview

Latin America has become one of the most attractive regions for remote workers and long-stay travelers, with several countries introducing dedicated digital nomad visa programs alongside existing temporary residence and extended tourist frameworks. The requirements and costs below reflect general program guidelines as of mid-2024 and may shift with regulatory updates. Always verify current conditions with the relevant embassy or immigration authority before applying.

## Visa Comparison Table

| Country | Visa type | Typical duration | Key requirements | Approx. cost | Processing notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Costa Rica** | Digital Nomad Visa (*Rentista Nómada Digital*) | Up to 1 year, renewable once | Proof of remote income ≥ USD 3,000/month; valid health insurance; clean criminal record; notarized employment/contract letter | USD 100–250 in government fees | Applications submitted through DGME (Dirección General de Migración); processing typically 2–4 weeks; dependents can be included under same application |
| **Colombia** | Digital Nomad / Remote Worker Visa (Visa Nómada Digital) | Up to 2 years | Proof of foreign-sourced income ≥ ~USD 684/month (3× minimum wage); active contract or client invoices; health insurance; passport valid 6+ months | ~USD 55 application fee + ~USD 177 issuance fee | Online portal application via Cancillería Colombia; biometrics required in-country; can accumulate time toward permanent residency after 5 years |
| **Mexico** | Temporary Resident Visa (*Residente Temporal*) | 1–4 years (renewable annually) | Proof of sufficient economic solvency (bank statements ~USD 43,000 savings or ~USD 2,600/month income); no dedicated nomad category but widely used by remote workers | USD 36–160 depending on duration tier | Must apply at a Mexican consulate abroad before entry; CURP registration required after arrival; no formal remote-work restriction |
| **Brazil** | Digital Nomad Visa (VITEM XIV) | 1 year, extendable by 1 year in-country | Proof of remote income ≥ USD 1,500/month or USD 18,000 in savings; valid health insurance covering Brazil; criminal background check apostilled | ~USD 65–130 consular fee (varies by nationality) | Introduced in 2022; apply at Brazilian consulate in home country; extensions filed with Polícia Federal; family members may apply concurrently |
| **Panama** | Short Stay / Extended Tourist + *Visa de Nómada Digital* | 9 months (short stay extensions) | Proof of income ≥ USD 3,000/month (USD 5,000 if bringing dependents); health insurance; no Panamanian employer; notarized contract or proof of business ownership | USD 250–300 in government and service fees | Panama's program launched 2021; processed through the Servicio Nacional de Migración; relatively straightforward but legal assistance (~USD 400–800) commonly recommended |
| **Argentina** | Rentista / Temporary Residence | 1 year, renewable | Proof of regular foreign income (bank statements or invoices); no strict minimum set by law but consular discretion applies (~USD 700–1,000/month recommended); health coverage | ~USD 200–350 consular and residency fees | No standalone digital nomad visa; *rentista* category most commonly used; peso inflation may affect fee amounts; Buenos Aires consulate generally efficient |
| **Ecuador** | Digital Nomad Visa (*Nómada Digital*) | Up to 2 years | Proof of remote income ≥ USD 1,350/month; foreign employer or self-employed with foreign clients; apostilled criminal record; health insurance | ~USD 50 application + USD 200–400 professional/legal assistance (optional) | Introduced 2023; processed at Ecuadorian consulates or in-country at Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores; one of the more affordable entry points in the region |

## Additional Considerations

Several of the visa categories above — particularly Colombia, Brazil, and Ecuador — allow holders to open local bank accounts and access short-term apartment rental platforms with greater ease than a standard tourist entry stamp. Tax obligations vary significantly: Costa Rica and Panama both operate territorial tax systems that generally exempt foreign-sourced digital income, whereas Colombia may impose local tax liability after 183 days of presence in a calendar year. Consulting a local immigration attorney and a cross-border tax advisor is strongly recommended before committing to any medium-stay arrangement.
