# Four-City Snapshot: Cost of Living Across Four Latin American Hubs

A rough, informal look at what your wallet faces in **Medellín**, **Florianópolis**, **Mexico City**, and **Buenos Aires** — all popular spots for remote workers, slow travelers, and transplants chasing a better exchange rate. Numbers are ballpark monthly figures in USD and will shift with exchange rates, neighborhoods, and your personal habits. Treat them as orientation, not gospel.

---

## Rent (1BR, Central-ish)

"Central-ish" here means walkable to cafés and transit — not necessarily the flashiest address, but not a two-hour commute either.

| City | Budget End | Mid-Range | Comfortable Upper |
|---|---|---|---|
| Medellín | ~$400 | ~$600–750 | ~$900–1,100 |
| Florianópolis | ~$550 | ~$750–950 | ~$1,100–1,400 |
| Mexico City | ~$500 | ~$700–900 | ~$1,100–1,500 |
| Buenos Aires | ~$350 | ~$500–700 | ~$850–1,100 |

Buenos Aires sits lowest largely because the peso situation makes USD-denominated budgets stretch well, though furnished expat listings priced directly in dollars can close that gap fast. Florianópolis trends higher than many expect — it's a beach city with serious domestic demand, and landlords know it. Mexico City's Roma and Condesa neighborhoods push toward the upper band quickly; venture toward Doctores or Narvarte and the mid-range becomes very accessible. Medellín's El Poblado inflates numbers; neighborhoods like Laureles or Envigado offer genuinely good value at the budget-to-mid tier.

## Groceries

For a single person cooking most meals at home, doing a weekly shop at a mainstream supermarket (not imported-goods specialty stores), expect something in this range monthly:

**Medellín** tends to run **$120–180/month** for solid, fresh-produce-heavy cooking. Local markets in neighborhoods like Paloquemao are remarkably cheap; imported snacks and wine push costs up noticeably.

**Florianópolis** comes in a bit higher, roughly **$160–230/month**, reflecting Brazil's broad consumer market but also some logistical markup for an island city. Meat, rice, beans, and tropical fruit are all very reasonable; European-style cheeses and imported goods are where you feel the pinch.

**Mexico City** is one of the more affordable grocery environments in this group — budget around **$110–170/month** if you lean into local staples: tortillas, dried chiles, legumes, seasonal produce from the mercado. The city's sheer size means competitive pricing at chains like Chedraui or Walmart, and traditional markets are plentiful.

**Buenos Aires** can be genuinely cheap for groceries when you're operating on dollars — roughly **$100–160/month** — with excellent beef, dairy, and wine prices that would make those same items feel luxurious elsewhere. Inflation erodes predictability in peso terms, but for dollar earners it remains a strong value.

## Transport

None of these cities demand a car for daily life, which is a meaningful cost advantage over many North American alternatives.

Medellín's Metro plus integrated bus and cable car system (the Integrado) makes it one of the best-value transit systems on this list — a monthly card runs around **$20–28**. Taxis and InDriver/Uber equivalents are cheap for occasional rides. Florianópolis is more car-dependent than the others given its island geography and sprawl; without a car, expect to rely on buses that are functional but infrequent in some zones, plus rideshares — budget **$40–70/month** depending on your neighborhood. Mexico City's Metro is famously inexpensive — a single ride is essentially pocket change, and a heavy-use month still rarely exceeds **$15–25** on the subway alone; add buses and occasional Uber and you're looking at **$30–60**. Buenos Aires has extensive Subte, bus, and train coverage; a Sube card for heavy transit use runs **$20–40/month** at current rates, with Cabify or rideshares adding more.

## Dining Out

This category varies wildly with taste. A casual sit-down lunch — the classic local midday meal — versus a proper dinner at a recommended restaurant are very different beasts.

A solid casual lunch (menú del día style) in **Mexico City** or **Medellín** will run **$4–8**. Buenos Aires surprises with great parrilla meals for **$8–14** at neighborhood spots. Florianópolis seafood lunches hover around **$10–16** for something genuinely good. For a proper dinner for two with drinks — think a place you'd recommend to a visitor — budget **$25–45** in Medellín and Mexico City, **$30–55** in Buenos Aires, and **$35–60** in Florianópolis. Coffee culture is strong in all four; a flat white or cortado runs **$2–4** across the board.

## Coworking

| City | Drop-In Day Pass | Monthly Hot Desk | Dedicated Desk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Medellín | ~$10–15 | ~$80–130 | ~$130–200 |
| Florianópolis | ~$12–18 | ~$100–150 | ~$150–230 |
| Mexico City | ~$15–22 | ~$100–160 | ~$160–250 |
| Buenos Aires | ~$8–14 | ~$70–110 | ~$110–170 |

Medellín has a dense coworking ecosystem in El Poblado and Laureles that keeps prices competitive. Buenos Aires undercuts the field again on dollar terms. Mexico City has premium options in Juárez and Polanco that push above these ranges.

---

## Home Internet

All four cities offer reasonably reliable fiber or cable broadband for residential use. Expect to pay **$25–45/month** in Mexico City and Medellín for a 100–300 Mbps plan. Florianópolis runs **$30–55/month** for comparable speeds through providers like Vivo or Claro. Buenos Aires sits around **$20–40/month** in dollar terms, though plan availability and speeds vary more by building and barrio than the other cities. Mobile data as a backup is cheap in Mexico and Colombia especially; Brazilian mobile plans are functional but pricier relative to the others.

---

*All figures are informal ballparks as of mid-2020s conditions. Exchange rates — particularly for Argentina — can shift the picture significantly in short periods.*
