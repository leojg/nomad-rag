# Medellín vs Mexico City Monthly Budget: A Real-World Cost Comparison

If you're weighing a move to Latin America — or just trying to decide where to base yourself for a season — Medellín and Mexico City sit near the top of almost every digital nomad and expat shortlist. Both cities offer a compelling mix of culture, infrastructure, and relatively affordable living compared to North American or European metros. But they're meaningfully different in how your money actually behaves month to month. Here's a rough breakdown across the categories that tend to matter most.

---

## Rent (1BR Central-ish)

Medellín's rental market has tightened noticeably over the last few years, largely driven by an influx of remote workers and increased tourism, particularly in neighborhoods like El Poblado and Laureles. That said, it still undercuts many comparable cities.

- **Medellín:** A furnished 1BR in El Poblado or a slightly better-value spot in Laureles typically runs somewhere in the **$500–$900/month** range. Go farther out to Envigado or Sabaneta and you might land closer to $400–$600.
- **Mexico City:** CDMX has seen its own rent surge, especially post-pandemic, with Roma Norte, Condesa, and Polanco becoming noticeably pricier. A decent furnished 1BR in those neighborhoods tends to fall around **$700–$1,200/month**, with some listings pushing higher. More local-feeling colonias like Narvarte or Del Valle can bring that down to $550–$800.

**Edge: Medellín**, though the gap has narrowed.

---

## Groceries

Both cities have a mix of large supermarket chains and local markets, and leaning into the latter makes a real difference in either place.

- **Medellín:** Shopping at places like Éxito or Carulla, plus supplementing at a plaza de mercado, a single person can realistically spend **$150–$250/month** on groceries. Produce is genuinely cheap, and local staples like beans, rice, and plantains keep costs low.
- **Mexico City:** Chedraui, Walmart, and La Merced market are the go-to options. Comparable spending lands around **$180–$280/month**. Imported goods and specialty items skew pricier, but the abundance of fresh tortillas, vegetables, and local produce keeps a budget-conscious shopper in good shape.

**Edge: Roughly even**, with a slight lean toward Medellín for produce variety and price.

---

## Transport

Neither city requires a car for comfortable urban living, and both have reasonably functional metro systems.

- **Medellín:** The Metro plus the cable cars and electric buses (integrated into the Civica card system) makes getting around straightforward and cheap. Monthly transit spending for regular use comes in around **$20–$40**. Occasional Uber or InDriver rides add maybe another $20–$30 depending on habits.
- **Mexico City:** The Metro is famously one of the cheapest in the world at a few pesos per ride, and Metrobús adds more coverage. A month of regular transit use might run **$15–$35**. However, many residents supplement with Uber more frequently due to safety considerations on certain lines at certain hours, which can push transport costs to $40–$70/month all-in.

**Edge: Mexico City** on pure transit cost, though Medellín offers a cleaner and more integrated experience.

---

## Dining Out

This is where both cities genuinely shine, and where you can spend almost nothing or a reasonable amount depending on your preferences.

- **Medellín:** A solid lunch at a local *restaurante corrientazo* — the classic set-menu lunch — runs about $3–$5. Dinner at a mid-range spot in Laureles might be $10–$20. Budget for dining out: roughly **$150–$350/month** depending on frequency and venue.
- **Mexico City:** Street tacos for $1–$2 a pop are a way of life, and sit-down lunch menus (*comida corrida*) hover around $4–$7. The sheer density and variety of the food scene is unmatched. Monthly dining spend can realistically be **$150–$400/month**, with the ceiling rising quickly if you're hitting the trendy spots in Roma or Juárez regularly.

**Edge: Mexico City** for value and variety at the lower end of the budget.

---

## Coworking

Both cities have mature coworking ecosystems catering to remote workers and startups.

- **Medellín:** Spaces like Selina, Atom House, or various independent spots around El Poblado and Ciudad del Río charge roughly **$80–$180/month** for a hot desk membership.
- **Mexico City:** A broader selection of spaces across colonias, with pricing generally around **$100–$220/month** for comparable access. Premium branded spaces like WeWork or Spaces push toward the higher end.

**Edge: Medellín**, modestly.

---

## Home Internet

- **Medellín:** Providers like Claro, Tigo, and ETB offer fiber plans in most urban areas. A reliable 100–300 Mbps plan typically costs **$25–$45/month**.
- **Mexico City:** Telmex (Infinitum) dominates, with Megacable and other providers filling gaps. Similar speeds run about **$25–$50/month**, though reliability complaints are somewhat more common.

**Edge: Roughly equal**, slight edge to Medellín on consistency.

---

## The Bottom Line

A comfortable but not extravagant month in **Medellín** for a single person might look like **$1,200–$1,900/month** all-in. The same lifestyle in **Mexico City** tends to run **$1,400–$2,200/month**, driven largely by higher rent in desirable neighborhoods and slightly elevated coworking and dining costs in the expat-popular zones. Neither city will break the bank by global standards, but Medellín still holds a modest overall edge for pure budget efficiency — while CDMX makes up for it in scale, food culture, and sheer urban energy.
